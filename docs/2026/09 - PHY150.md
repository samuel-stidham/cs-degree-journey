**Course Description:**
In this algebra-based physics course, students will explore the major fundamental topics in physics as they relate to mechanics, such as motion and forces, gravity and projectiles, and energy and work. Through their exploration of these topics and embedded lab work, students will learn to describe the motion of objects in both one and two dimensions, and to solve problems through the application of Newton’s Laws of Motion. Additionally, they will also apply the principles of the conservation of energy and momentum to analyze the behavior of interacting objects.

---

## Key Topics & Concepts

This algebra-based mechanics course builds a foundation in classical physics through paired theory and embedded lab work:

* **Kinematics:** Describing motion in one and two dimensions using distance, displacement, speed, velocity, and acceleration.
* **Newton's Laws of Motion:** Applying the three laws (inertia, F = ma, action-reaction) to analyze forces acting on interacting objects.
* **Gravity & Projectile Motion:** Modeling free-fall and projectile trajectories by decomposing motion into independent horizontal and vertical components.
* **Work & Energy:** Calculating kinetic and potential energy, and applying the work-energy theorem to mechanical systems.
* **Conservation Laws:** Applying conservation of energy and conservation of momentum to predict outcomes of interactions such as collisions.
* **Foundational Competencies:**
  * **PHY-20656:** Describe the motion of an object in one and two dimensions.
  * **PHY-20657:** Apply Newton's laws of motion to solve problems.
  * **PHY-20658:** Apply principles of conservation of energy and momentum to analyze the behavior of interacting objects.

## Major Projects & Assignments

All three projects were framed as deliverables for a simulated client, "A&L Engineering," requiring a diagram, a step-by-step kinematics/dynamics analysis, and a written explanation of the physical reasoning behind each equation used.

1. **Project One: Supply Drop Plan (Objects in Motion)**
    * **Focus:** Two-dimensional projectile motion for an aerial supply drop, analyzed by treating horizontal and vertical motion as independent components.
    * **Application:** Calculated fall time, horizontal displacement, and resultant impact velocity for a payload released from a plane at 250 mph and 2,650 m, then combined the velocity components via the Pythagorean theorem.
    * **Scenario Adaptations:** Re-ran the analysis under two changed conditions: a real-world headwind (sourced from a live National Weather Service report) that reduced horizontal velocity and shortened the drop distance, and a catapult launch at an angle, which required resolving the launch velocity into components with sine/cosine before determining whether the trajectory cleared a target height.

2. **Project Two: Case Material Evaluation Report (Newton's Laws)**
    * **Focus:** Applying all three of Newton's laws to a phone-drop test comparing protective case materials.
    * **Application:** Modeled a 2.0 m drop in two phases, free fall (using the time-independent kinematic equation to find impact velocity) and impact (using collision time to find deceleration and Newton's second law, F = ma, to find impact force), across four cases: no case, silicone, hard plastic, and rubber.
    * **Conclusion:** Identified the rubber case as offering the best protection, since its longer collision time produced the lowest impact force (20.9 N) despite adding the most mass, then weighed that result against real-world trade-offs like bulk, grip, and cost.

3. **Project Three: Roller Coaster Design Report (Energy and Momentum)**
    * **Focus:** Tracking the interplay of potential energy, kinetic energy, and momentum across a roller coaster track, then extending the analysis through a collision and a friction-based stop.
    * **Application:** Calculated PE and KE at five track points (launch, two valleys, two peaks) to confirm that total mechanical energy stays constant when friction is ignored, then modeled a perfectly inelastic collision between two carts using conservation of momentum to find the post-collision velocity and the kinetic energy lost to heat, sound, and deformation.
    * **Extension:** Applied the work-energy theorem to a final friction-based stop, calculating the frictional force required to bring the combined cart to rest over a fixed distance, and tied each energy "loss" back to conservation of energy by accounting for where the energy actually went.

4. **Lab: Kinematics**
    * **Focus:** Building graphical and physical intuition for position, velocity, and acceleration across five short activities.
    * **Application:** Interpreted a position-vs-time graph of a train that sped up, stopped, and reversed direction. This distinguished total distance traveled from net displacement and identified graph slope as instantaneous/average velocity. Used an inclined-ramp experiment with steel and acrylic spheres to confirm that displacement is proportional to time squared under constant acceleration (a linear distance-vs-time² plot) and that an object's mass has no effect on its acceleration due to gravity.
    * **Observation-Based Confirmation:** Used a ramp with evenly and unevenly spaced rubber-band markers to audibly confirm constant acceleration: equally spaced markers produced sounds that sped up over time, while markers spaced according to the d = ½at² relationship produced a steady, evenly timed beat.

5. **Lab: Momentum**
    * **Focus:** Verifying the law of conservation of momentum across three cart-collision trials with varying mass distributions (equal masses, added mass on Cart A, added mass on Cart B).
    * **Application:** Calculated system momentum before and after each collision from cart velocities measured by hand-timed trials, finding percent differences between the theoretical and measured values ranging from 15.3% to 31.1%.
    * **Error Analysis:** Attributed the discrepancies to friction and human reaction-time error in the sub-second timing measurements rather than a failure of the conservation law, and explained how a reversed (negative) post-collision velocity for the lighter cart in one trial was handled by preserving the vector sign of momentum throughout the calculation.

## Discussion Work & Academic Themes

1. **Newton's Third Law: Squid Jet Propulsion**
    * **Focus:** Used squid locomotion as a real-world action-reaction pair to illustrate Newton's Third Law.
    * **Analysis:** Explained how a squid draws water into its mantle cavity and expels it backward through the hyponome, a muscular funnel acting as a nozzle. The expelled water is the action force. The equal and opposite reaction force from the water pushes the squid forward. Emphasized the often-missed nuance that the action and reaction forces act on two different bodies (squid and water), not the same object. That is the mechanism that lets the squid accelerate.

2. **Conservation of Energy: From Power Plant to Pixel, and a Battery's Inner Life**
    * **Original Post Focus:** Traced the full energy transformation chain behind a gaming PC, from chemical energy in coal/natural gas burned at local power plants, through grid transmission and AC-to-DC conversion in the power supply, to consumption by the CPU/GPU and peripherals. Nearly all of that energy ends as waste heat. Concluded that energy is always conserved, but becomes progressively less *useful* as it disperses, distinguishing conservation from usefulness.
    * **Instructor Follow-Up:** Asked for a diagram of an AA alkaline battery's internal components, an explanation of why batteries degrade over time, and whether a battery's energy should be considered "conserved" given that it eventually dies.
    * **My Response (Extended the Analysis Further):**
        * **Original Diagram:** After finding that reference battery diagrams were copyrighted, built an original Excalidraw cross-section of an AA alkaline cell labeling the steel can, cathode (manganese dioxide), separator, anode (zinc paste), KOH electrolyte, brass collector pin, and terminals, citing DoITPoMS for component identification.
        * **Battery Degradation:** Explained that a cell "dies" because its zinc and manganese dioxide reactants are consumed during discharge, raising internal resistance until the cell can no longer sustain voltage. This is a depletion of usable chemical reactants, not a loss of energy.
        * **Conservation Argument:** Argued the battery's total energy remains conserved under the First Law of Thermodynamics: chemical potential energy converts to electrical energy and then to thermal energy without ever disappearing, only becoming less useful as it disperses (Urone & Hinrichs, 2022, Section 15.1).
        * **Pushed the Reasoning Further:** Proactively raised and answered a follow-on question: whether cooled, dispersed heat is still conserved. Concluded yes, since cooling is a transfer to the surroundings, not a destruction of energy, and tied the declining *usefulness* of dispersed heat to entropy and the Second Law of Thermodynamics (Urone & Hinrichs, 2022, Section 15.6).
    * **Takeaway:** The response moved beyond the original post's qualitative energy-tracing exercise into a sourced, two-law argument that cleanly separated the conservation of energy from its usefulness. It applied the same reasoning from the PC example to a real chemical system, then extended it one step further than the instructor's question asked.

## Personal Reflection & Takeaways

My view of physics shifted significantly over the course of this term. I went in expecting the math to be a barrier, carrying old assumptions that formulas and dense terminology like "entropy" put the subject out of reach. That assumption didn't hold up. Once I slowed down and worked through the steps, the math was approachable, and once I understood what a term like entropy actually described (a measure of a system's disorder), the intimidation around it faded. That pattern repeated for nearly every concept in the course.

The course also changed how much physics I notice in everyday situations. Flying to a work conference in Lincoln, Nebraska, midway through the term, I caught myself tracing all three of Newton's Laws through a single flight: the thrust needed to overcome the plane's inertia at rest, the second law governing how much force is needed to accelerate or decelerate that much mass, and the third law showing up twice: once in the engines, where exhaust expelled backward pushes the aircraft forward, and again in the landing gear's action-reaction pair on touchdown. Before this course, none of that would have crossed my mind.

The two concepts that stuck with me most were Newton's Laws (especially the elegant simplicity of F = ma and the action-reaction pairing I explored in a discussion post about squid propulsion) and thermodynamics: the idea that energy is never created or destroyed, only converted, and that each conversion increases disorder (entropy) and makes the energy less useful over time. Working through both ideas left me with a new interest I didn't expect going in: making physics more approachable for people who get scared off by the math or the vocabulary before they ever reach the underlying concept, in the spirit of communicators like Carl Sagan and Neil deGrasse Tyson.
